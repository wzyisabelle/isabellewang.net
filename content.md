## Shawshank in Keywords
description:
When machines “see,” what exactly do they perceive?
This project explores how machines interpret emotionally and narratively rich visual content. Using the CLIP model, 34,206 frames extracted from The Shawshank Redemption were analyzed and matched with keywords from a neutral dictionary of 5,000 words. The result is a reconstructed version of the film—seen through the cold, impartial gaze of a machine—where meaning emerges not through plot, but through patterns of recognition, abstraction, and misinterpretation.

## Emo Frame
description:
Who or what gets to define the subtleties of how we feel?
EmoFrame is a wearable device that materializes the digital gaze of machine vision. A transparent acrylic frame, cut in the shape of the bounding boxes used by facial recognition systems, sits in front of the wearer's face. When activated, a webcam feeds into a DeepFace model that classifies the wearer's expression in real time; an Arduino-driven LCD screen mounted on the frame displays the identified "emotion" and its confidence score for others to read.
The work sits in the loop between two acts of perception, one human, one algorithmic. It asks what happens to emotional communication when a machine's reading is inserted between two people, and whether the externalized signal brings us closer or further apart.

## Electric Touch
description:
What does it mean to touch in a world where contact is purely visual?
Electric Touch is a browser-based interactive installation that responds to a single hand tracked through the webcam. Pinching and dragging gestures let users pick up floating pixel blocks sampled in real time from their own fingertips, which then drift, pause, and vanish from the screen. The hand-tracking data is intentionally mirrored, creating an illusory hand that mimics but resists the user's own, turning a seemingly simple act of touching into a negotiation between vision and imagination.
The work sits in the blur between signal and sense, between the body and its double, asking what becomes of intimacy, friction, and contact when only vision remains.

## Pieces of Time
description:
Under the division of time zones, what does our life look like in each other's eyes?
This browser-based work maps the day into four colored blocks representing sleep, work, personal time, and preferred hours for connection. By entering their own schedule and time zone, users see their daily rhythm rendered as a moving composition, and can view someone else's life unfolding in parallel on the other side of the world. The link was shared with friends across continents, whose responses came back as unique visual traces, weaving a soft connection through fragmented hours.
